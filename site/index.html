Chapter one — the wait

For weeks, students sat at Jantar Mantar with one question: who leaked our exam?

A sit-in over the alleged NEET-UG paper leaks had been running for weeks — aspirants who did everything right, asking why the system didn't. On 18 July, activist Sonam Wangchuk — roughly twenty days into a hunger strike beside them — was removed from the site by police and taken to hospital. corroborated

Sources on every claim: the sourced timeline.

Chapter two — 20 July, morning

Parliament's Monsoon Session opened. The city answered a march with a fortress.

Permission for the march was denied. Prohibitory orders went up across central Delhi. Over 5,000 police personnel were deployed; metro stations — Rajiv Chowk, Janpath, Patel Chowk — were closed. corroborated

By 7:30 am, per ThePrint's live blog, lathis had already been used near the Park Hotel — before the march had formally begun. claimed

Chapter three — 10:04 am

The charge.

As marchers moved from Jantar Mantar toward Parliament, police lathi-charged the crowd and fired tear gas near the Parliament premises, Shastri Bhawan and Raisina Road. corroborated

Police, via PTI

The action followed stone-pelting and barricade breaches by protesters.

Marchers, via Al Jazeera & Daily Pioneer

The march was peaceful when the lathi-charge began, suddenly.

Two versions exist. That is precisely why evidence — with time, place and chain of custody — matters more than anyone's press briefing.

Chapter four — the blackout

Then the phones died.

Mobile internet was suspended across parts of central Delhi — reportedly a ~5 km radius around Jantar Mantar. As of the Internet Freedom Foundation's statement that evening, no legal suspension order had been published by any authority. corroborated

A blackout doesn't erase footage. It only delays it. The clips recorded in that window are still on phones across this city — waiting to be preserved.

Chapter five — the unbadged

Look closely at the uniform. Something is missing.

Newslaundry's photo report documented police personnel without name badges — before the protest had even begun — and personnel in plain clothes carrying lathis. claimed — one photo report so far

A name plate is not decoration. It is the one thing that makes a uniform answerable to the public it polices. When it is removed before a deployment, accountability doesn't disappear — it moves up the chain of command, to whoever sent that unit out unmarked.

One photo report is a claim. Your footage is what turns a claim into a corroborated fact. That is the entire reason this archive exists.

Chapter seven — the record

What they un-badged, we index.

Every original file that reaches this archive is fingerprinted the moment it arrives — SHA-256, write-once, an append-only custody ledger built to stand in court. Every public claim carries a status label: claimed corroborated. Nothing is published as fact before it earns it, and no one is named guilty here — courts do that.

Officers who removed their identification are not anonymous. They are unidentified — so far. Clip by clip, marker by marker, the record grows.
